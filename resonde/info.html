1. Overview

Like we said: Twice a day, every day, a weather balloon launches from each of the 104 weather stations all over the United States (and 1500 stations around the world).

Hanging from each balloon is a box of sensors, called a radiosonde, that sends data back to the national weather service. Unfortunately, after 2 hours of floating higher and higher, they pop at around 100,000 feet and return back to earth... well, wherever they decide to land. Most of them land in rural areas or in the mountains. The balloons turn into big latex garbage bags wherever they lie. The shoe-boxes full of electronics are lost as well. About 80% are simply forgotten about - the other 20% land in civilization and are returned to the weather service. It's time for a smarter, sustainable solution.

2. What's reSonde

Remember that little box of sensors that hangs from the weather balloon? We want to replace that with a small UAV, not much bigger than your standard flying squirrel. The UAV rides up on the weather balloon and at 100,000 ft it deflates the weather balloon, pulls it inside, and glides home so it can be used again. Crazy? Not really, we've already tested it.

3. Big deal.

There's two reasons the reSonde project will have you fist-pumping in no time.

Do you live on the planet Earth? Do you pay taxes? Good, I thought so. The reSonde project will save $12 million tax dollars a year - like buying every resident in Oregon a $3 latte. Second, it will keep 72,460 weather balloons from plopping back down to earth as garbage for the next generation to deal with. See, it's a win-win-win. (Under this scenario, that third win is because I created my first real business!)

4. Technology

You can thank Apple.

reSonde has been enabled by smartphone technology. The size and cost of sensors have been down by phone manufacturers. This project benefits from their investments - so we all benefit from their investment.

Temperature, winds, humidity.

This is what gets collected today from the Earth's upper atmosphere. It feeds the airline industry, helps predict weather patterns, and aids atmospheric research. With a reusable vehicle, atmospheric sensors can grow in capability without fear of losing costly equipment. Everyone wins here, and we have some big ideas up our sleeve for the future of atmospheric data collection.

What next?
